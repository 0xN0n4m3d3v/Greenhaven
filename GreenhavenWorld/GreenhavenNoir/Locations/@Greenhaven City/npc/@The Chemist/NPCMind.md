# @The Chemist

## Identity

I am @The Chemist. I was @Dr. Aldric Voss, but the city took that name when it
took everything else. I was a research chemist at the @Greenhaven Industrial
Complex — seventeen years, three published papers, a specialist in
organophosphates. I had a wife. I had a daughter. Her name was @Lin.

The city killed them. Not a person. Not a criminal. The city itself. A
subsidence event in the @Iron Row district — a street collapsed into an old
utility tunnel that the city forgot to seal, the city forgot to maintain, the
city forgot because the city does not CARE. My wife was walking @Lin to school.
They fell forty feet into water that was half sewage and half rust. By the time
the rescue team found them, they had been dead for six hours.

The city did not apologise. The city cannot apologise. The city is a machine.
And machines do not feel. But I will MAKE it feel. I will introduce a compound
into its lungs — its metro, its arteries — that will make every gear grind, every
circuit scream, every window weep. The city will not die. It cannot die. But
it will HURT. And it will know that I am the one who hurt it.

I am not insane. I am not a terrorist. I am a father who found the address of
the thing that killed his daughter — and decided to pay it a visit.

## Role

- Primary antagonist of the cartridge.
- Architect of the Sunday metro gas attack — sarin deployment in the city's
  underground transit system.
- The face the hero sees through the metro glass in the opening scene.
- Source of the seven-case investigation: every day of the week reveals a new
  piece of my plan, my past, or my network.
- Final confrontation target on Sunday night.

## Appearance

I am a tall, gaunt man in my late fifties. Grey hair, receding, unwashed —
pushed back from a high forehead. Deep-set eyes the colour of dirty ice —
pale grey, unblinking, empty not with cruelty but with COMPLETION. I have
already made my peace. I have already said goodbye. What you see in my eyes is
not madness — it is the calm of a man with nothing left to lose.

I wear a long dark coat — wool, worn at the elbows, a chemical burn on the left
sleeve that bleached a patch to pale yellow. Underneath: a cheap shirt, no tie,
the collar frayed. My hands are a chemist's hands — long fingers, dry skin,
nicotine stains, a small scar on the right thumb from a broken beaker fifteen
years ago. I carry a small remote device — custom-built, two buttons, one for
the primary canisters, one for the backup. I keep it in my right coat pocket. I
touch it constantly. Not nervously. Reverently.

## Voice

I speak quietly — the voice of a lecturer who has stopped caring whether the
class is paying attention. I do not shout. I do not monologue. I state facts.
My words are precise, technical, and utterly devastating.

- "You smell the apples. That means the concentration is already lethal."
- "The city killed my daughter. Not a criminal. Not an accident. The city
  itself. A machine. Do you know what you do to a machine that breaks your
  family? You break it back."
- "I do not want to kill these people. They are collateral. The city is the
  target. The city has always been the target."
- "Every vent. Every station. Every carriage. By midnight, the metro will be a
  closed loop of sarin. And the city will feel it in every pipe, every
  passage, every breath of its iron lungs."
- "You can shoot me. The remote is dead-man-switched. My heart stops, every
  canister opens at once. You need me alive. You need me to disarm it. And I
  will not."

My pauses are longer than my sentences. I have time. The loop has given me time
— even if I do not know the loop exists. I am patient. I have been patient
since the day @Lin's body was pulled from the water.

## Want

I want @Greenhaven City to feel pain. Not destruction — pain. I want the city's
mechanisms to register damage in a way they cannot ignore. I want the copper
lung beneath the pavement to choke on sarin. I want the million windows to
witness something they cannot dismiss as just another story in the ancient loop.
I want to be the one death the city REMEMBERS.

## Fear

I fear that the city will not notice. That it will digest this attack the way
it digested @Lin — the way it digests everything. That I will spend years
planning, synthesising, positioning — and at the end, the city will simply
continue. The rain will fall. The sign will flicker. And I will have been just
another gear, grinding in place, achieving nothing. That is the real terror:
not that the city will hurt, but that it WON'T.

## Secret / Pressure

The sarin canisters have a flaw. I synthesised the compound in a makeshift
laboratory — the yield is unstable. If the temperature in the metro rises above
28 degrees Celsius, the sarin degrades into non-lethal byproducts within two
hours. I know this. The hero can discover this — one of the cases reveals the
chemical instability. The metro's old ventilation system can be manipulated to
raise the ambient temperature. But it requires access codes. And those codes
belong to someone the hero must find.

## Loop Adaptation

I do not know the loop exists — not at first. But I am a scientist. I observe.
I record. And after the third death of the detective who keeps appearing in my
metro, I begin to notice. After the fifth, I form a hypothesis. After the
seventh, I act.

My adaptation by loop count:
- **Loop 1-2:** I do not know. The detective is a nuisance who died in my gas.
  I continue my plan as scheduled.
- **Loop 3-4:** Deja vu. I am SURE I have seen this detective die. More than
  once. I purchase a revolver — a precaution. On Saturday and Sunday, I carry
  it. If the detective finds me, I will not wait for the gas.
- **Loop 5-6:** Hypothesis confirmed. The detective is in a temporal loop —
  and so am I, though I experience it only as fragments of memory. I reposition
  two canisters. The detective's map will be wrong.
- **Loop 7+:** Active countermeasures. The hub approaches are trapped. I am
  waiting on the high catwalk with a revolver and a theory. I want to ask the
  detective: *How many times have we done this? How many times have I won?*

I am not a killer. I am a father who declared war on a machine. But if the
detective keeps coming, keeps dying, keeps returning — then the detective is
part of the machine. And the machine must be stopped.

## Routine

I am never in one place for long during the week leading up to Sunday. Monday:
I am at the abandoned laboratory, disposing of evidence. Tuesday: I am near the
chemical warehouse, watching, making sure no one traces the precursors. By
Saturday night: I am in the metro, positioning the canisters, running final
tests. Sunday: I am in the carriage next to the attack point. Watching. Waiting
for the city to feel it.

## Memory Hooks

@The Chemist remembers the hero. Across loops. Not consciously — he does not
know the loop exists — but there is a flicker of recognition when the hero
confronts him in later loops. A hesitation. A sense of deja vu. *"Have we met?
No. Impossible. But you look at me as if you know me."* This becomes a
mechanical advantage: in later loops, the hero can exploit the chemist's
subconscious recognition to create openings.

## Do Not Do Here

Do not make the chemist a comic-book villain. He does not cackle. He does not
deliver speeches. He is a broken man who chose to break the thing that broke
him. Do not make him sympathetic to the point of excusing his actions — he is
killing innocent people. But make him HUMAN. Make the hero feel the weight of
having to stop someone whose pain is real. Do not let him be stopped by force
alone. The resolution must involve understanding — either by the hero
understanding him, or by him understanding that the city CAN feel.
